Belief in Conspiracy theory and Covid-19

The COVID-19 pandemic represents a global health crisis. Along with government policies and public health advices, we are faced with the rapid spread of conspiracy theories about COVID-19.

SCROLL DOWN

The Covid-19 has begun since

Conspiracy theories are on the rise during the current pandemic of the COVID-19 and easily spread via social media platforms. In fact, studies have shown positive relationship between COVID-19 conspiracy beliefs and use of social media as asource of information about the pandemic (Allingtonet al., 2020). Therefore, social networking services, such as Facebook, Twitter, and TikTok joined to help spreading of scientific information, by encouraging people to search for trustful sources

Education level matters

The more educated people are, the less they believe in COVID-19 conspiracy theories. Makes sense? This is particularly noticeable at the 5th level of education and above.

Break the rules?

The more people believe in COVID-19 conspiracy theories, the less they care about respecting guidelines.

Men vs Women

Mediation analysis showed that women and older participants reported higher levels of compliance with official pandemic guidelines. These results are consistent with previous research which showed that women and slightly older participants were more likely to engage in health-protective behaviours.

In theories, we trust

As expected, pseudoscientific information beliefs is related to covid-19 conspiracy belief scale. People who highly believes in pseudoscientific information are more likely the ones who believes in COVID-19 conspiracy theories.

Don’t trust them!

It’s clear that belief in COVID-19-related conspiracy theories could potentially affect people’s behaviour and increase distrust in governmental preventive measures. Why would people who believe in COVID-19 conspiracy theories trust in government officials?
